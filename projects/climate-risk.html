Cost-Benefit Analysis of Urban Green Infrastructure

A comparative research report synthesizing public cost data, contractor estimates, and planning literature across seven urban green infrastructure categories to build a more usable decision-support reference for urban planning.

Project Overview

This project asked a practical planning question: if cities want to invest in urban green infrastructure, what does implementation actually cost across different intervention types, and how usable is the public cost information that planners rely on?

Working from a natural capital framing, I compiled and standardized cost information for seven urban green infrastructure categories: tree installation, lawn installation, bioswales, greenways/trails, rain gardens, green roofs, and vertical greenery systems. The end product was a comparative report designed to make fragmented cost information more legible for planning and research use.

Why This Matters

Urban green infrastructure is often discussed in terms of benefits: cooling, stormwater management, carbon capture, habitat, and public health. The harder question is implementation. Cost data are often scattered across contractor websites, local reports, academic studies, and one-off planning documents, which makes comparison difficult and decision-making uneven.

I wanted to build a clearer baseline. Not a universal price list, but a structured reference showing how costs vary by category, what assumptions sit behind those numbers, and where the evidence is strongest or weakest.

Approach

Defined seven categories

I grouped urban green infrastructure into seven broad project types so the analysis could compare like with like across the report.

Collected mixed-source cost data

The dataset combined books, scientific literature, university resources, contractor websites, public estimators, and direct outreach where possible.

Standardized units and assumptions

Costs gathered in different formats, currencies, and units were normalized into a common reporting structure so categories could be compared more meaningfully.

Audited source quality and gaps

Beyond the cost summary itself, the report tracked which sources appeared most often and where regional or methodological blind spots remained.

Data and Methods

Geographic coverage

I combined national averages with state-specific information from Washington, Texas, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Illinois, Indiana, Georgia, North Carolina, New York, and Massachusetts.

Source types

Inputs came from library books, scientific databases, university research pages, contractor websites, public price estimators, and expert outreach.

Standardized project sizes

To make categories more comparable, I assigned reference scales such as one 10-foot tree, per-square-foot lawn installation, a 2,500-square-foot bioswale, a 3-mile greenway/trail, and standardized sizes for rain gardens, green roofs, and green walls.

Normalization decisions

Currency conversions and unit conversions were standardized, but I intentionally did not fully inflation-adjust the dataset because location, design intent, and implementation context were still major drivers of variation.

Benefit framing

Each category was paired with qualitative benefit and drawback notes, so the report did not reduce the decision purely to a dollar-per-unit comparison.

Output structure

The final deliverable included category-level cost summaries, source attribution, a standardized comparison table, and a section documenting remaining knowledge gaps.

Findings

Cost is highly category-dependent

The report showed that urban green infrastructure cannot be treated as a single cost class. Installation, site preparation, and maintenance profiles vary dramatically across categories.

Public data are fragmented and uneven

Some categories had relatively strong public sourcing, while others relied on sparse or project-specific estimates that made standardization much harder.

Planning context changes the math

The report reinforced that project location, intended use, design choices, and maintenance assumptions can all shift total cost as much as the category itself.

One of the more useful conclusions was that combining green infrastructure with planned infrastructure improvements can reduce costs substantially, with several source materials pointing to savings in the 30–60% range.

Limitations and Reflections

The most important limitation was not analytical complexity. It was data access. Much of the public cost information was old, incomplete, highly local, or buried in formats that made direct comparison difficult. Contractors also often required a real project address before sharing pricing information, which limited direct cost validation.

I also found clear geographic bias in the available data. The Northeast was better represented than the Midwest and Great Plains, which means the resulting comparison is informative but not evenly distributed across the U.S. landscape.

What made this project valuable to me was learning how to make a messy evidence base more usable without pretending it was cleaner than it really was. The report is strongest as a structured decision-support reference, and future work could improve it with newer contractor data, stronger regional coverage, and more systematic treatment of maintenance assumptions over time.
