Projects / Barrier Island Shoreline Change Detection

Remote Sensing & Machine Learning

Barrier Island Shoreline
Change Detection

Time-series analysis of multispectral satellite imagery to quantify decadal shoreline migration rates across Georgia's barrier island chain, using an automated random forest classification pipeline in R.

Overview

Georgia's barrier islands — a chain of 15 major islands between the Altamaha Sound and the Savannah River — are among the most dynamic coastal landforms on the U.S. East Coast. Understanding how their shorelines are migrating is essential for habitat management, sea turtle conservation, and coastal resilience planning.

This project quantified shoreline change rates for seven islands using Landsat 5/7/8/9 and Sentinel-2 imagery spanning 1985–2023, and contributed to a peer-reviewed publication on coastal change detection methodologies.

Classification Pipeline

A random forest classifier was trained in R to distinguish water, wet sand, dry sand, and vegetated dune from multispectral imagery. Training data were manually digitized for four Landsat scenes spanning different seasons and tidal states. The classifier was applied to all available cloud-free imagery in the archive (> 400 scenes), with tidal correction applied post-classification using NOAA gauge data.

Shoreline positions were extracted as the wet/dry sand boundary, then projected onto shore-normal transects at 25-meter intervals using the Digital Shoreline Analysis System (DSAS) toolbox in ArcGIS.

Results

  • Detected net accretion on the northern ends of three islands and net erosion on two southern termini — consistent with dominant northward littoral drift.
  • Average long-term change rate: +0.3 m/yr (accretion) on north-facing headlands, −0.6 m/yr (erosion) on south-facing spits.
  • Post-storm (Hurricane Matthew 2016, Hurricane Irma 2017) shoreline pulses were quantifiable within 2–3 Landsat overpasses, demonstrating sub-seasonal detection capability.
  • Random forest classification achieved overall accuracy of 91% on held-out validation scenes (F1: water 0.97, dry sand 0.88, vegetation 0.86).

Publication

Findings contributed to a peer-reviewed publication examining the accuracy and scalability of automated shoreline extraction from multispectral satellite imagery, with particular attention to micro-tidal environments typical of the Georgia Bight.
