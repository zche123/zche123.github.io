Sea Turtle Nesting
Habitat — Jekyll Island

A multi-variable habitat suitability model for loggerhead sea turtle (Caretta caretta) nesting on Jekyll Island, Georgia, integrating LiDAR-derived beach morphology, satellite vegetation indices, and 13 years of tide-corrected shoreline position data.

Background

Jekyll Island hosts one of Georgia's most significant loggerhead nesting beaches. Female loggerheads exhibit strong site fidelity, returning to the same stretch of beach where they were born decades earlier. As shoreline dynamics shift under increasing storm frequency and sea-level rise, understanding which beach segments offer stable, high-quality nesting habitat becomes critical for conservation planning.

Despite 30+ years of nesting surveys, no spatially explicit suitability model had been constructed for Jekyll. This project aimed to fill that gap by fusing field-survey records with publicly available geospatial datasets.

Data Sources

  • NOAA Topobathymetric LiDAR (2016, 2020) — dune crest elevation, beach width, slope
  • Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery (2018–2023) — NDVI for dune vegetation density
  • USGS CoastSat shoreline positions (2010–2023) — 13 years of seasonal shoreline
  • DNR Jekyll Island nesting survey records (2010–2023) — 1,200+ nest locations
  • NOAA tidal datum corrections — MHHW-referenced shoreline normalization

Methods

Beach morphology was derived from LiDAR point clouds processed in ArcGIS Pro. Dune crest elevation, foredune face slope, and dry beach width were extracted along 20-meter cross-shore transects at 10-meter alongshore intervals.

Shoreline positions were extracted from Sentinel-2 imagery using the NDWI water index, then corrected to mean higher high water datum using NOAA tidal predictions for the Brunswick gauge. Year-over-year shoreline change rates were computed for each transect using end-point rate (EPR) and linear regression rate (LRR) methods.

NDVI was computed from Sentinel-2 bands 8 and 4, aggregated seasonally (March–May, pre-nesting) to capture vegetation density at the time of nest-site selection. Values were averaged across 20×20 m grid cells aligned to the transect network.

A logistic regression model was trained on nest presence/pseudo-absence data (n = 1,200 nests; 1,200 matched random absences in non-nested zones) with predictors: dune crest elevation, beach width, foredune slope, NDVI, and 5-year shoreline change rate. Model performance was evaluated with 10-fold cross-validation (AUC = 0.81).

Key Findings

  • Dune crest elevation (> 1.8 m MHHW) and beach width (> 30 m) were the strongest positive predictors of nest presence.
  • High NDVI (dense dune vegetation) negatively associated with nest success — turtles avoided heavily vegetated sections.
  • The northern 2.4 km of the island showed net accretion (+0.4 m/yr) and consistently high suitability scores; the southern terminus showed net erosion and declining scores across the study period.
  • Suitability mapping identified a 700-meter priority conservation corridor where planned dune restoration would have the highest marginal benefit per dollar invested.

Outputs

Deliverables included a continuous suitability raster (10 m resolution), a transect-level summary geodatabase, and a technical report submitted to the Georgia Department of Natural Resources Coastal Resources Division. Results are being incorporated into the Jekyll Island Conservation Authority's 5-year beach management plan.
